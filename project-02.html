The POC Guy

One Block Away LLC · Founder & AI Product Engineer · 2023–Present

I turn early client AI questions into working POCs they can test, pitch, and use to decide what is worth building next.

The Ask

These projects started when a client had an AI idea, a business pressure, and no clear proof that the idea would work. The real question was, "Can this exist in a form we can see and react to?"

My job was to translate that question into a tight product surface, make the smallest useful version, and build enough behavior to separate real opportunity from wishful thinking.

The Answer

I treated each POC as a decision tool. It had to show the workflow, expose the hard parts, and give the client something specific to approve, challenge, or change.

That meant product strategy, UX, AI workflow design, data assumptions, front-end build, backend logic, and demo readiness all lived in the same loop. No handoff chain. No waiting for a full team before the client could see whether the idea had legs.

POC Builds

Two client-led POCs, built from "is this possible?"

The common thread was speed with enough rigor to be useful. The output was not just a prototype screen. It was a working first version that made the next product conversation sharper. Each one needed its own shape, because each client question was different.

POC 01 · Real estate AI

ListingPal

A client wanted to know if an agent could enter basic property details and get a credible listing campaign fast enough to matter while the opportunity was still warm.

Client ask

Can address, price, and a few property details become a usable marketing package without forcing agents to stitch together five different tools?

What I built

An AI listing-generation POC plus a public landing page for the product. The product flow generates listing descriptions, social posts, ad copy, interior styling direction, and MLS/SEO-ready language in under 90 seconds. For a deeper dive, the long-form ListingPal walkthrough shows the full flow in context.

Why it mattered

The POC moved the agent from blank-page writing to fast review and revision. The landing page turned the prototype into something explainable: a product story, demo path, beta positioning, and a clear way for interested agents to understand the value.

AI model

The landing page frames ListingPal around AgentSelect, a model-orchestration layer that chooses specialized LLMs for specific real estate tasks instead of relying on one model for every output.

POC 02 · Startup intelligence

WeReady

WeReady helps founders turn startup uncertainty into an evidence-backed readiness picture: what is credible, what is risky, and what to fix next.

Client ask

Can a founder submit a repository or startup context and get a useful, evidence-backed view of technical risk, business signals, funding readiness, and product experience?

What I built

A Bailey Intelligence dashboard that turns founder inputs, GitHub signals, government sources, academic research, and investor frameworks into a structured startup-readiness report.

Why it mattered

The POC moved WeReady from generic startup advice into a product surface clients could inspect: source health, credibility signals, risk scoring, and next-step recommendations.

AI model

Bailey organizes the product into code, business, investment, and design intelligence flows, with semantic search framed as the next source-linked interface.

WeReady treated analysis as a product surface: Bailey turns live sources, scoring logic, evidence trails, and recommendations into founder-ready next steps.

  • Built the content model around live source coverage, not static AI answers.
  • Separated Bailey into code, business, investment, and design intelligence workspaces.
  • Used source health, credibility scoring, and evidence trails as visible product features.
  • Positioned semantic search as the next layer: ask Bailey and get source-linked answers.

Build Pattern

The format was always the same: clarify the question, build the answer.

A POC only matters if it answers the business question that created it. I started by cutting each ask down to the smallest version that could prove or disprove the idea.

For ListingPal, that meant proving speed and quality could work together in a real estate workflow. For WeReady, it meant proving startup readiness could be scored and explained across technical, business, investor, and UX dimensions.

I kept the work close to the client question. No sprawling feature set, no fake enterprise roadmap, no abstraction for its own sake. The demo had to show the actual product move.

That is where I am most useful: turning "could this work?" into an artifact a client can click, critique, and decide from.

What This Proves

What client-led POC work actually proves.

  • Client translation I can take an unclear ask, find the real decision underneath it, and turn that into a product surface people can respond to.
  • POC scoping The work stayed bounded. Each build answered a specific question instead of pretending to be a finished platform on day one.
  • AI workflow design ListingPal and WeReady both required more than prompting. They needed intake structure, scoring logic, output review, and product judgment.
  • Design and build together The interface and backend moved together because the same person owned the flow, the AI behavior, and the demo experience.
  • Speed with judgment Fast did not mean careless. The goal was to make a useful first version quickly enough that the client could decide what was worth building next.
  • Demo readiness The output was built to be walked through, questioned, and adapted. That is the point of a POC: make the next conversation specific.
Reflection

A good POC should reduce uncertainty, not create theater.

These projects worked because the client question stayed visible the whole time. I was not trying to prove that AI was exciting. I was trying to prove whether a specific product workflow could be useful enough to continue.

That is the lane I want this page to show: clients bring me the "is this possible?" moment, and I turn it into the first version they can actually judge.
