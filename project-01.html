Joined as the second design hire. Left three years later with a 30-person team, an award-winning design system, and a working process that scaled across 22 brands and 7,000+ daily users.

The Challenge

No design function, no shared language, 22 brands doing their own thing. Every analytics product in the IPG portfolio was built separately — different patterns, different components, different visual logic. Users bouncing between tools felt like they'd switched companies.

The Solution

A token-based design system built from scratch: 500 components, 140 styles, 300 custom icons, 50 research-derived personas. One codebase serving 22 brand identities without forking. Adopted across engineering teams, not just handed off to them.

Key Challenges

What we were up against.

  • Engineering owned every product decision

    20+ products, all built by engineers making design calls. Not because they wanted to — because there was no one else. Users paid for it. Usability was an afterthought, if it appeared at all.

  • 22 brands, zero shared patterns

    Each product had grown independently. Getting them to use the same system meant winning buy-in from separate engineering teams, product owners, and clients — none of whom had asked for this.

  • No user feedback in the existing system

    The previous attempt at consistency was built in a vacuum. Nobody had asked users what was broken. We had real data showing the navigation alone was costing people significant time every week.

  • The system had to outlast the project

    A design system that needs a dedicated maintenance team is already failing. The goal was adoption so thorough that teams would contribute back — which meant governance and documentation had to be built in from the start, not bolted on later.

Solutions Overview

How we actually built it.

Four things drove most of the decisions. Not principles — specific choices that either worked or didn't.

Atomic structure

Built from the smallest possible units up. Atoms into molecules into organisms. This made 22-brand theming tractable — you change a token, not 500 components. It also meant engineers could use the system without waiting on design for every variation.

One shared language

Shared components meant a button was a button everywhere. Sounds obvious. In practice it required negotiating with engineering teams who had built their own versions and didn't want to throw them out. The argument that won: consistency reduced their support burden, not just ours.

Research before pixels

We ran interviews before drawing anything. Found out navigation was costing users real time every session. Found out color customization was being done manually in PowerPoint after export. Both of those became features, not just design decisions.

Documentation that teams actually used

Most design system docs get written once and ignored. We kept ours close to the engineering workflow — versioned, linked from tickets, updated when things changed. When teams started filing requests against it rather than building around it, we knew it was working.

The Challenge

No design function. No shared language. 22 brands doing their own thing.

When I arrived, Kinesso was engineering-first. There was no design practice. Every analytics product in the IPG portfolio was built separately, each with different patterns, components, and visual languages. Users bouncing between brands felt like they were using completely different products.

The ask was simple: fix this. The work turned out to be organizational change dressed as design.

The Approach

Build the team, then build the system. Not the other way around.

Most design system projects start by building components. I started by hiring people. Brought in the first UX researchers, the first interaction designers, the first people who knew how to think about visual systems. We understood what was broken before we drew anything.

Then we went through every product in the portfolio. Mapped out what patterns existed, where things overlapped, and built a token system that could handle 22 different brands without forking the whole codebase.

The system included governance, contribution models, and adoption playbooks. We worked directly with engineering teams to integrate it rather than just handing it off.

Research Impact

What users actually told us — and what we built because of it.

While the UI team was building components, I ran interviews across product teams and end users. Five findings shaped the first release.

System-Wide Navigation

Research Finding

Users were seeing the full product menu every time — but most only needed 3 or 4 tools for their actual work. The rest was noise that made it harder to find the things they used daily.

Design Impact

Navigation now shows only the products each user actually has access to and uses. Users can pin and reorder their tools. Nobody explained this change with a tooltip — they just noticed things were easier.

Global Color Picker

Research Finding

Users were exporting charts, then manually recoloring them in PowerPoint to match their clients' branding. It was a known workaround that everyone had accepted as normal.

Design Impact

Built a global color picker directly into the platform. Users can apply a client's brand colors to an entire data page in one step, save presets, and never open PowerPoint to fix a chart again.

Design Excellence

Research Finding

As the team grew and more people were contributing designs, stakeholders started asking how we'd know when something drifted from the system. Good question — we didn't have an answer yet.

Design Impact

Built the Design Bot — a proprietary Chrome extension that audits designs against the system's components and templates, then generates a report with specific flags and fixes. Compliance became checkable, not just aspirational.

Personalization

Research Finding

Users wanted control over how data was displayed — not just what data appeared, but how they saw it. The platform had one default view for everything. That worked for nobody particularly well.

Design Impact

Customizable profile experiences: users can set preferred views, adjust how dashboards display data, and save configurations per client. Beta users got productivity shortcuts first, which gave us signal on what to make permanent.

White Label Automation

Research Finding

Every new enterprise client wanted a branded version of the platform. The process for delivering that was manual, slow, and relied on individual designers who knew the system well enough to not break anything.

Design Impact

Built the White Label Automation Builder — a sequential tool that walks product leads and client teams through creating a custom-branded instance. The system handles token application automatically. No designer required to spin up a new client.

The team grew from two designers to 30 people across New York, Chicago, Los Angeles, and Warsaw. I built the hiring process, defined career levels, set up design reviews, and created the operational rhythm.

Every hire was deliberate. We filled gaps in research, interaction design, visual systems, and accessibility. By year two, design had a voice in product decisions for the first time.

The real lesson: hire designers to shift how the organization thinks about product quality, not to fill open slots.

The Results

What shipped, what stuck, what won.

  • 7,000+ daily active users The entire IPG analytics portfolio on one system. Users moving between brands felt the same product, not 22 different ones.
  • 22 white-label brands from one codebase Token-based theming meant each client got their brand without anyone forking the repository.
  • 3 Gold, 2 Silver — Indigo 2023 UX Interface & Navigation, Digital Tools & Utilities, Graphic Design, Interaction, Interactive Design.

Reflection

What I'd tell someone doing this for the first time.

Building a design system is easy. Getting engineers to believe it's worth the investment is hard. That meant showing up to every sprint, every architecture call, every planning session, whether design was supposed to be there or not.

The system succeeded because adoption was easier than the alternative. We didn't mandate it. We just made the default path the best one.

If I did it again, I'd push harder on contribution models earlier. The system worked best when teams owned their components, not when they were just using ours.

The awards were nice. But the real win was watching engineering teams file feature requests against the design system instead of building their own patterns. That's when you know it's actually working.
