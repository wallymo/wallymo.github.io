The Thesis

The best way to learn AI is to ship AI.

After leaving Kinesso, I didn't jump to another corporate gig. I started shipping things. Real products with real constraints, not experiments or polished demos. The reasoning was straightforward: if I'm telling companies how to do AI, I should have actually built AI products.

One Block Away was my testing ground. Each product meant the full cycle: find the problem, design it, architect it, build it, launch it, support it. Solo. No funding rounds or co-founders.

What Shipped

Three platforms. Each solving a different problem.

Multi-Agent Coordination

WeReady

A platform that routes tasks to specialized AI agents instead of throwing everything at one model. Orchestration isn't just wrapper code — it's a design problem that changes what the product can actually do.

Real Estate AI

ListingPal

Generates complete real estate listings in 90 seconds. Photos, descriptions, pricing, neighborhood breakdown. What took hours before, finished while the agent is still in the driveway.

Workflow Automation

BriefFlow

I built this because agencies kept asking the same question: how do you wrangle a hundred half-baked briefs into something you can actually execute? Takes a messy brief and turns it into structured work. Tasks, timelines, who does what. Simple as that.

"The best founders I know don't just have ideas. They ship things that work."
— The operating principle behind every project
Beyond Products

The infrastructure you don't see.

The products you can see are half the story. The other half is the infrastructure underneath. It's the same thinking applied to systems instead of features. How do things actually work when nobody's watching?

Multi-agent framework that assigns work to different AI models depending on what the task needs. A newsletter system that researches, writes, and publishes every day across different topics without touching it. A trading system that reads signals, executes strategies, and manages risk on its own.

None of this was built to look impressive. It was built to break in specific ways so I could fix them. Every system taught me something about architecture, reliability, and what separates a working prototype from something people actually use.

Building something you use yourself is the only way to learn what building for others actually takes. Your newsletter crashes at 4am, you learn monitoring. Your bot has a bad day, you learn guardrails. Stakes are real. Learning sticks.

What This Proves

A single founder who can do all of it.

  • Product Thinking Identifying real problems, scoping solutions, and making hard tradeoffs without a PM telling me what to build.
  • Design Execution Every interface designed and built by the same person who architected the backend. No handoff gaps.
  • Technical Architecture Multi-agent orchestration, RAG pipelines, trading algorithms, automated content systems. Production, not prototypes.
  • Operational Maturity Monitoring, alerting, rollback procedures, knowledge graphs, structured logging. The stuff that keeps products alive after launch.
  • Speed Concept to production in weeks, not quarters. Solo doesn't mean slow — it means no coordination overhead.
  • Judgment Knowing when to ship good enough vs. when to invest in polish. Knowing when to use AI and when not to. The hardest skill to hire for.
